Samuel Mead (Beg4Cred) is a prolific artist, who lives and breathes creativity. His work is not confined to any single mode, style or technique, instead being characterised by the expression that emanates from everything he creates. Samuel is involved in a number of different projects built out of his DIY approach, including Quantum Couriers, The New Earth Art School and The Lurner Prize. Stepping outside of the norms and systems presented to us in order to construct new, more open ways of solving problems, interacting with one another and expressing ourselves.

Samuel spoke to us about a range of topics, giving insight on everything from his creative process, to the joys of interacting with the people he meets during his free courier service.

To keep up with Samuel’s art, sign up to his newsletter by emailing him at [email protected], or follow him on Instagram. You can also follow Quantum Couriers, The New Earth Art School and the Lurner Prize. Interview by James.

Abbreviated transcription below, listen to the interview for the full discussion!

James: So, how did you start out making art?

Samuel: Since I was young I’ve always been into making art. I was encouraged to draw, paint, things like that. I got really inspired by the impressionists. I remember being taken to see an exhibition and seeing Monet in particular made me want to create something that has that kind of power, and I never really stopped you know?

J: Yeah definitely, I was gonna say that you very quite prolific in your output…

S: Very much so, I see making art as being integral to my life as anything else. It’s just something that I do, the same way I eat every day I make art every day and it’s been that way for quite a few years. It’s a natural thing for me to live creatively and I always think about making stuff, I don’t really separate my art from the rest of my life.

J: I was wondering if you had any core philosophies or ways you approach making art?

S: I suppose there’s lots of ways to come at this question. I approach art as a natural part of my everyday life, I think art is a way of learning about myself, and a form of therapy. It’s a process of self-realisation, I make art because I’m trying to express myself and through that process of self-expression you are able to learn who you are, I think that’s important. I have very firm beliefs in the idea that all of us are essentially creative beings and that’s something I strive to embody every day, to be a creator in every moment.

J: It seems that you don’t really limit yourself in modes of expression and the mediums you use, that you approach art in a lot of different ways.

S: I just like making stuff, I’m not really interested in any kind of definition of what I’m doing. One day I might decide to write a poem, well actually one hour I might be writing a poem and the next I might a song. It’s all expression, I feel like I’ve got a lot to give and it’s difficult to just pick one mode to do that, I just feel very free in trying everything and I love it.

J: It seems that some of your pieces must have taken a while to make, while some look like they could have taken seconds…

S: That relates to what I was saying about trying lots of different things. There are times when I feel like I want that meditative feeling and do a really involved, incredibly detailed pen and ink drawing, and then some days there’s more of a feeling of wanting to do things quickly and just get them out there.
I say that but there both meditative for me in different kind of ways, Its just based on how I feel.

J: In terms of influences, do you have any others that come to mind ?

S: I’m inspired in lots of different ways by lots of different people, artists like Monet and Picasso….. recently artists like Hilma af Klint, who was a real foundational artist that didn’t get the credit she deserved.

The way I approach art is definitely inspired by musicians I admire too. I loved Hip-hop growing up and the DIY entrepreneurial spirit of it has always inspired me. The way that a lot of rappers would just create their own brand and become successful by themselves… that classic image of people selling CDs out of the back of their car, that really inspires me and is the way I want to sell my art, it’s almost like a hustler approach to art. It suits me as I make so much; I’ve got all this product. In terms of trying to make a living as an artist, its helpful to me to have that kind of inspiration.

The actual artistic style that I have is clearly inspired by the great artist of the 20th century and I feel like that’s really ingrained in my work. Then in more recent years as I’ve tried to get my work out into the world I’ve started to take inspiration from people who aren’t necessarily artists, people who have found a way to make their mark on culture, which is something I’ve really wanted to do. I feel really passionate about bringing the work I do into a mainstream context. I feel like the ideas that I represent have been on the fringes for too long, I don’t embrace the term “Outsider art” as I want to be a mainstream artist, but without having to compromise anything important and bring that philosophy in front of more people.

J: Could you talk about your project Quantum Couriers ? How did you come with the idea for that? I imagine its kind of close to the philosophy of putting out ideas by embodying them.

S: Its all one thing really. I’ve always been into cycling, I used to be a courier and I loved cycling around the city and meeting people. Being in that environment and contributing to that environment in the form of street art… although the job itself was quite brutal and I did feel like I was exploited and mistreated by the company I worked for. During the first lockdown I wanted to do something with the courier bike I still had and do something to help, and also just to get out and connect with people. So I thought id just offer this service with the bike and see if anyone needs help moving things around.

I came into contact with people who were handing out food and asked if they needed help, that has now become the core thing of what I do. So at the moment I’m collecting hot meals in Covent Garden and ride around, finding people who might need food and giving it out. I’ve been doing it for a few months and have a regular run and have started to get to know people who I often see. I wanted to do something that I thought could be helpful to people but also something that I would enjoy. I get to have all the aspects of being in the city while contributing something that people might really need.

I’m trying to demonstrate that you can still do things in ways that haven’t been thought of before. For example I don’t really consider what I do charity; I don’t really like the word. Charities sometimes operate in ways which I don’t really agree with, there tends to be a dynamic of the helpers and the people being helped. For me we’re all the same and all equally deserving of a good life, some people are just in a difficult situation. I like to interact with people on an equal level which I think can mean a lot to people who are often looked down upon. It’s important for me to demonstrate that it is worth doing things in life that aren’t necessarily paid for.
