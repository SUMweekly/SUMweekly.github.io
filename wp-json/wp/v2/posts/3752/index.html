    Making waves in the underground London art scene, we sat down with Jaffar to discuss everything from how he got started in art, to his upcoming \u201cBlack Angst\u201d exhibition –  a show that delves into the murky waters felt by black people asked to assimilate into a system that cannot compute ones existence.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    \n
    \n

    Have you always wanted to be an artist?<\/strong><\/p>\n\n\n\n

    I don\u2019t recall stumbling across peers like myself in my early years of education that actually wanted to be an artist or even spoken openly about always wanting to be an artist. Our school was filled with children of the diaspora or had parents from the diaspora. So, you can already imagine how we are conditioned at home to strive for \u201ceconomically stable\u201d jobs that allow us the \u201ccoloured\u201d, \u201cOther\u201d individual to be accepted in our journey of assimilation in this Babylon. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Same goes for the state schools we went to. Everyone in that school is just trying to pump you out, and that is it. But there are always those teachers who do add an element of care. And it did spark a level of want for art. But at the end you are funnelled through the hole they want you exiting. I only felt comfortable being an artist when I started to see others like myself.  And that was only near the end of my undergrad in Anthropology.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n\n\n\n
