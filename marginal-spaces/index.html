Article / Marginal Spaces

July 25, 2021July 25, 2021 Articles James Farrant

Within the vast, modern city of Shenzhen in China, Baishizhou sticks out like a sore thumb. As one moves into it, the meaning of the modern city is completely subverted.

From the uninhabited open expanse, into the tight, noisy, populated streets you are immediately brought into a space that feels like a genuine reflection of the inhabitants that reside within it.

Shenzhen is a city largely defined by those who possess ownership of it. The city is sharply demarcated by each construction firm and their own goals; from one block to another one finds oneself within vastly different meanings of what the city is.

In this, Baishizhou represents a counter narrative to the modernity the city prides itself on, and as a consequence, is under threat.

Shenzhen is fascinating for this very reason; its rapid growth has resulted in its very streets becoming a case study in the evolutions of modern cities.

Shenzhen originated as a small collection of fishing villages. During the city’s exponential boom in the ‘70s, the CCP brought the land from the fishermen; however, due to their homeownership, they held on to the rights to occupy the land. As a result, the fishermen were granted greater agency over their pockets of land within the city. As the city grew it enveloped these areas of land, and a stark contrast emerged between the spaces which were owned by massive national firms, and the collectivisation era properties, owned by comparatively small enterprises [1].

These urban villages came to play a big role in the city’s tech boom, providing affordable housing for the city’s low-income workers, nearby to the factories that produce the goods that put Shenzhen on the map [2]. In its prime, 150,000 people inhabited Baishizhou, the largest of these villages [3].

Recently however, Baishizhou, and the hundreds of other urban villages, have been under threat. Their numbers have been shrinking. Since 2005 the government has been closing down parts of Baishizhou [4], and in 2017 plans were announced for a redevelopment of the entire site [5]. In 2019 it was reported that its 150,000 residents had been asked to leave by September of that year [6].

Since my visit to Shenzhen in 2018, with mass evictions in 2019, the population of this village has shrunk considerably, with entire streets being closed off [7]. It seems the fate of this space is sealed.

The conflict between these areas in the city manifests the moment and context from which they arise. People’s lives are subject to the overwhelming power of capitalism, and the city that it creates (china’s economic reforms are expressly capitalist, with the majority of land and productive power being owned by private entities [8]).

These urban villages came about as marginalised spaces within a wider vision for a modern, construction firm led cityscape. For a period of time, they were able to exist for their usefulness in facilitating the city’s economic boom in the new technological era, providing affordable homes for migrant workers coming from the country’s rural areas in search of work.

The quasi-organic form these spaces took formed a powerful counter narrative to the premeditated city, which surrounds it. Densely populated and full of life, they sit in stark contrast with the atomised navigation of space that takes place in the rest of the city.

For the construction firms of the city, these plots of land represent massive financial incentive and opportunity. With property prices being exponentially high within the city, the potential profits of a redevelopment project are huge [9].

And with no one in power to defend the interests of these residents, they are forced out into the surrounding province of Guangdong. It seems that the convenience of these places of living was considered too high for the low-income residents of the village, amongst the wealthy megacity.

Ultimately, Baishizhou only existed, as a fragment of the cities disjointed metamorphosis: from a small fishing village of just 30,000 to a megacity of 12.59 million in just 40 years [9][10] – a process of growth leading to the end point of a total hegemony of space, and marginalisation of the working poor.

This process highlights fundamental dynamics in Chinese capitalism. China has been moving rapidly from a production oriented LEDC, to finance and business oriented MEDC, with a massively expanding middle class.

As such, less need for low paid workers has led to the further marginalisation of the working class. Despite this, Chinas economy still has a massive reliance on its productive capacity, and the inevitable move to the homogenous form of the city, causes direct harm to the companies that have defined the city’s tech boom [9].

“If Shenzhen loses its affordable housing, the whole chain will break. The industrial sector will hollow out, and for most of our start-ups, our design companies, and our tech companies, the cost of labour will go up, because almost all of their young workers live in the urban villages.” – Local Architect; Duan Peng (SCMP, 2016)[9]

With less housing for low paid workers, these companies face a shortage of employees. Here we can see how the state steps in to resolve the inherent flaws that arise from unregulated capitalism. In 2018 the CCP announced its solution; by 2035, 60% of its new housing supply would be government-subsidized. Currently, state-backed homes account for just 5% of the market [10].

Ultimately, these urban villages represent clearly how space, and the life that inhabit it, relate to forces beyond their control. It demonstrates a microcosm of the flux of life within cities; as people seek out work and a means to a good life, alongside the conflicts this gives rise to.
